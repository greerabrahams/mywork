Thought Leadership

You are what you repeat

Why showing up beats showing off.

By Greer Abrahams

Liked and forgotten, then liked and semi-remembered, then liked with a chuckle, until you're a household name. Think of it as a bonfire versus a furnace. The bonfire is the campaign. It's spectacular, everyone gathers round, somebody takes a photo. And by morning it's a pile of ash and a scorch mark on the lawn. The furnace isn't a show. Nobody gathers to watch it. It just burns, quietly, unattractively. When we break it down to its core, the unattractive furnace is the very thing keeping the house warm through the cold months. Recognition is earned through repeated, consistent showing up, not through one clever tagline, one pretty picture and one over-the-top gift voucher. Say less, do more. It's about how consistently you show up, not how you perform.

A spectacular performance only lasts so long, and is unsustainable as a long-term marketing strategy. There are only so many fun gimmicks and breathtaking campaigns you can run. Your always-on content is what carries you. Don't neglect it. Give it its rightful place in the ecosystem. After all, it's the gift that keeps on giving if done properly.

Marketers, even the really good ones, tend to see always-on content as the boring thing that needs to be done, rather than the lifeblood of the marketing engine. We're excited about the brand campaign, the sizzle, but what about the steak? Like a bunch of five-year-olds on a soccer field all running after the ball, leaving no one to defend the goalposts. Everyone loves brand campaigns, but have you ever stopped to think whether all the excitement, all the hard work, all the late nights are really worth it? And don't even get me started on the upfront financial outlay. Take a beat and ask yourself honestly: how many brand campaigns have actually led to sustained results?

Campaigns, while fun and notorious for quick wins, are short-lived. The hype will wear off, and if you have nothing to support it with, well, you're pretty much screwed. Too many brands live campaign to campaign and, quite frankly, it's exhausting. You're tired, the audience is tired, and still the mistakes pile up. Lather, rinse, repeat. Literally. We all thrive on consistency. A campaign every now and then, yes. One nice giveaway, yes. But you are in serious trouble if you're relying solely on campaigns to carry you through.

Here's where this shows up in the wild:

The eager start-up, hungry and sure they've cracked it, sprints for the deep end and does one spectacular bellyflop for the crowd, then finds they're too winded to swim the length. It's the most natural mistake in the world. The grand entrance feels like progress. But starting slow and keeping it going, the unsexy path, is the one that actually works. Looking grand and delivering on it are not the same thing. Consistent, high-quality content marketing is what keeps you in the water long enough to compete with the brands who've been swimming for years.

And the legacy brand, asked to move from traditional marketing to digital and quietly wondering when the rules changed. It's a fair question. Traditional marketing never demanded a steady stream of content. It was mostly push, and we rarely heard back from the customer, because reaching us was hard. Great for businesses, less so for everyone else. We're nearly the opposite now. People are informed, they have options, they have spending power, and they can talk back. The game genuinely changed, and you can't simply copy the old playbook into the new one.

So, understandably, the scramble to keep up: one brand campaign after another, chasing relevance. But here's what gets missed in the rush. These brands already have the thing a start-up would give anything for. Years of earned trust. A furnace, already lit. And we leave it sitting there, unattended, while we head out to the lawn to build another bonfire.

Strip it back and the start-up and the legacy brand made the same mistake. One never learned to keep showing up. The other forgot. The spectacle is always tempting, and the bonfire always draws a crowd. But the brands that last are the ones still there in the morning, doing the unglamorous thing again, and again, and again.

If, in your assessment, you've concluded that you are in fact surviving from campaign to campaign, you have a small but distinct window of opportunity to turn it around. Go to your whiteboard, whip out your Sharpies, bring out the highlighters, and don't forget the sticky notes. Never forget the sticky notes. We've got work to do! Ask the questions you asked yourself when you first started:

Who are you?

What do you do?

How do you do it?

What are your goals?

How do you show up?

Start at the very beginning. It's a very good place to start!

Because you are what you repeat, and what carries a brand is the repeated, unglamorous showing-up, not the campaign firework. The way you approach always-on content is what moves it from the boring part that you have to do, to something that deserves your full and intentional attention. A strong always-on strategy only lands when it's spaced and varied. It dies when it becomes mechanical and predictable. Since you're in the driver's seat, and only you know your brand, let the data guide you. Don't be afraid to test. It's okay to fail at first, as long as you pivot effectively.

Learn who you are, learn who your audience is, and then show up for them consistently. You are what you repeat.
